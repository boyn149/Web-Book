The Difference Between a Bad Prompt and a Good Prompt

 

The secret to getting the most out of ChatGPT is learning how to ask the right questions, otherwise known as prompts. A bad prompt can lead to vague or irrelevant responses, while a good prompt will produce the insightful, precise answers you're looking for.

For instance, let's say you're writing an article about the impact of AI on the job market.

Bad Prompt:

You:

"Tell me about AI and jobs."

 

ChatGPT:

"AI is having a significant impact on the job market. As artificial intelligence technologies become more advanced, they are automating tasks previously done by humans, leading to changes in the workforce. Some jobs are disappearing, while others are being created. AI can also improve efficiency and productivity, resulting in new opportunities for businesses and workers."

 

While the response is accurate, it's generic and doesn't offer any unique insights or specific information about the job market.

Now let's try a better prompt:

You:

"Can you provide me with three specific examples of how AI is changing the job market for freelancers in the creative industries within the next five years?"

 

ChatGPT:

"Certainly! Here are three ways AI is expected to change the job market for freelancers in creative industries within the next five years:

- Personalized marketing strategies: AI-driven analytics can help freelancers in the creative industries develop highly targeted marketing strategies based on consumer behavior and preferences. This will enable them to create more effective campaigns, ultimately attracting more clients and increasing their income.

Freelancers in these industries can leverage these tools to create stunning visuals more quickly and efficiently, opening up new opportunities for collaboration and innovation."

 

By providing specific instructions, context, and a clear goal, the good prompt elicits a much more detailed and informative response that directly addresses the question at hand.

The Limitations of ChatGPT

 

It's not perfect, and being aware of these shortcomings can help you make the most of its capabilities.

Memory

 

While ChatGPT can process and analyze vast amounts of text, it has a limited amount of text it can hold in its memory at a given time. It's a bit like trying to remember a long shopping list – the more items you add, the harder it gets to recall everything.

So, what does this mean for you as a user?

Well, if you're working on a lengthy piece or asking a series of interconnected questions, ChatGPT might lose track of the context. To overcome this challenge, you might need to break down your questions or instructions into smaller, more manageable chunks.

Forbidden Topics

 

ChatGPT is programmed to avoid engaging in conversations that involve illegal activities, violence, or other harmful or sensitive subjects. This safeguard ensures that the AI doesn't accidentally promote harmful content or misinformation.

The downside?

ChatGPT may sometimes be a bit of a scaredy-cat, steering clear of certain topics that might be perfectly legitimate in context. To avoid running into this roadblock, try rephrasing your questions or instructions to focus on more neutral aspects of the topic.

Knowledge Cut Off

 

ChatGPT's knowledge has a cutoff date. At present, it's September 2021.

That means any events, advancements, or new information that have emerged since then won't be part of its knowledge base.

It literally can’t talk to you about them, because it doesn’t know they happened.

Otherwise, though, it knows everything published on the web before September 2021. Its knowledge is astonishingly vast.

Accuracy

 

Occasionally, ChatGPT gets the facts wrong or even invents them entirely. To make matters worse, it’s convincing when it “hallucinates” these facts, talking to you about them with complete confidence.

Personally, I’ve only noticed it once or twice after thousands of hours of use, so I don’t think it’s anything to worry about, but if you’re working on any research-driven projects where the accuracy of the data is essential, it’s a good idea to double check it.

Common Mistakes Beginners Make When Crafting Headlines

Let’s go through some examples and talk about how to avoid them.

Writing vague or generic headlines

 

You've seen them before - headlines so generic, they could be describing any article on the internet.

And they are super easy to ignore. They're like a weak handshake or a limp piece of lettuce - no one wants to engage with them.

For example, imagine an article with the headline "Tips for Better Communication." It's not exactly attention-grabbing, is it?

But what if we reworked it into something more specific, like "7 Unconventional Ways to Instantly Improve Your Communication Skills"? Now we're talking!

This is because it offers a clear benefit to the reader. All the best headlines do.

Focusing on the Method Instead of the Outcome

 

Sometimes, writers get so caught up in explaining how their ideas work that they forget to tell readers why they should care.

It's like trying to sell someone a vacuum cleaner by describing its motor and suction power, instead of simply saying it'll make their carpets spotless.

And it’s a huge mistake.

Your readers are more interested in the benefits and results they'll get from your article, rather than the intricate details of the methods you're describing.

Take this headline, for instance: "Using the Pomodoro Technique for Time Management." Sure, it tells you what the article is about, but it doesn't tell you why you should care.

A more effective headline might be "How the Pomodoro Technique Can Help You Conquer Procrastination and Get More Done."

What gets your readers fired up? What stirs their emotions and makes them want to share your content with everyone they know?

The opposite is also true.

When your headline evokes a strong emotion, it's more likely to grab your reader's attention and get them to engage with your content.

For example, a headline like "The Real Reason You're Stuck in a Dead-End Job" taps into the reader's feelings of frustration and dissatisfaction, making them more likely to click and read your article.

How to Choose the Best Headline

 

So, you've brainstormed a list of potential headlines for your article, and you're staring at them, feeling overwhelmed.

How do you pick the one that's going to reel in readers and make the biggest splash?

Simple - focus on your desired outcome. In this case, though, we are talking about YOUR desired outcome, not the reader.

But what exactly is a "desired outcome" for a content writer?

It's the primary goal you want to achieve with your article. And in the world of content marketing, there are three main outcomes you should consider: traffic from search engines, traffic from social media, and converting readers into buyers.

The catch? You can only choose one primary outcome for each headline.

Let's dive into each of these outcomes and how to assess your brainstormed headlines to find the perfect one.

Traffic from Search Engines

 

When your goal is to attract traffic from search engines like Google, you need to prioritize keyword optimization. That means including the main keyword your target audience is searching for in your headline.

For instance, let's say you're writing an article about time management for busy parents. Your primary keyword could be "time management tips for parents." A search-optimized headline might look like this:

"10 Time Management Tips for Busy Parents: Get More Done in Less Time"

 

Notice how the headline is clear and directly relevant to the topic. Readers searching for time management advice for parents will instantly recognize that this article is for them.

Traffic from Social Media

 

When you want your article to go viral on social media platforms like Facebook or Twitter, your headline should evoke emotion.

A headline that sparks curiosity, excitement, or even controversy is more likely to be shared, liked, and commented on.

For our time management example, a headline optimized for social media might be:

"How to Transform Bedtime from Hectic to Heartwarming: The Busy Parent's Guide"

 

This headline is focused on the emotional journey of the reader, making it much more likely to get shared.

Converting Readers into Buyers

 

In this case, you'll want to create headlines that focus on the product categories or solutions. The idea is to pique your readers' interest in what you're selling, highlight the benefits, and provide a clear path to purchase.

Note: this is most useful for affiliate-driven websites and e-commerce stores.

When crafting headlines for this outcome, consider the following:

1.   Showcase your product categories: Make sure your headline highlights the specific products or services you offer. This helps potential customers understand the scope of your business and how it can benefit them.
 

Example: "10 Must-Have Kitchen Gadgets for Busy Parents: Save Time and Cook with Confidence"
 

2.   Emphasize benefits: Use your headline to convey the advantages your products or services provide. This can help readers see the value in making a purchase and entice them to learn more.
 

Example: "Discover the Secret to Effortless Time Management: How Our Planner Helps Busy Parents Stay Organized"

Assessing Your Brainstormed Headlines

 

Now that you know the implications of each desired outcome, it's time to assess your brainstormed headlines. Start by reviewing your list and categorizing them based on the three primary outcomes: search engine traffic, social media traffic, and conversions.

Ask yourself: Which of these headlines would work best for my desired outcome? Are they optimized for search engines, emotionally engaging for social media, or persuasive enough to convert readers into buyers?

For example, let's say you have these headlines:

"How Busy Parents Can Master Time Management and Win Back Their Lives"

 

"10 Must-Have Kitchen Gadgets for Busy Parents: Save Time and Cook with Confidence"

 

"Time Management for Parents: Stop Drowning in Chaos and Reclaim Your Sanity"

 

Headline 1 might be a great option for search engine traffic, with its clear and relevant focus on time management for busy parents. Headline 3 has an emotional appeal, making it more suitable for social media traffic. And headline 2 emphasizes the product categories, making it most suitable for conversions.

Making the Final Decision

 

By now, you should have a better understanding of which headline will best serve your desired outcome.

Keep in mind that while it's important to optimize for a primary outcome, it's also essential to strike a balance between the three. A headline that only focuses on one outcome may not be as effective as one that considers all three to some extent.

Remember, your headline is the first impression your content makes on your audience. By choosing the best headline for your desired outcome, you increase the chances of your article getting noticed, shared and, ultimately, achieving its goal.

AI can’t do that for you. Not yet anyway.
